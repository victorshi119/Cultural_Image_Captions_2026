# GUARANI (AVAÑEʼẼ) — Image-caption generation cheat-sheet
# Target register: STANDARD LITERARY PARAGUAYAN GUARANI.
# Default sentence length: one short clause, descriptive present tense.

================================================================
A. ORTHOGRAPHY (every character matters for evaluation)
================================================================
- Vowel inventory: oral a e i o u y; nasal ã ẽ ĩ õ ũ ỹ.
  (y = high central vowel /ɨ/; ỹ is its nasal counterpart.)
- Consonants: p t k kuʼ mb nd ng ñ r s h v y (and rr, j, l in loans).
  Nasal consonants: m, n, ñ. Velar nasal written as g̃ in some words (hag̃ua, g̃uahẽ, ñag̃ uatĩ).
- Puso (apostrophe ʼ) is a glottal stop and a real character. Always include:
  haʼe, peteĩ→peteĩ, ñeʼẽ, kaʼaru, koʼág̃a, mbaʼe, raʼy.
- Stress: default stress is on the FINAL syllable. When stress is non-final,
  the stressed vowel carries an acute accent: á é í ó ú ý. These are characters.
  Examples: kuéra, ára, téra, óga, ára, hára.
  An accent disappears when the word is suffixed and stress shifts:
    óga ‘house’ → ogape ‘at home’ (NOT *ógape).
    téra ‘name’ → terakuéra ‘names’.
  Heuristic: when adding a stressed suffix (-kuéra, -hára, -ʼỹ, -ʼi, -ánte, -ára),
  the stem usually loses its acute accent.

================================================================
B. NASAL HARMONY (THE single highest-leverage rule)
================================================================
A stem is NASAL if it contains any of: ã ẽ ĩ õ ũ ỹ ñ m n (anywhere in the stem).
Otherwise it is ORAL. Affixes attached to a nasal stem switch to nasal allomorphs:

   AFFIX             ORAL FORM   NASAL FORM   EXAMPLES
   plural det         -kuéra      -nguéra       jagua-kuéra / mitã-nguéra
   locative           -pe         -me           óga-pe / tetã-me / ñu-me
   emphatic           -ko         -ngo          upéa-ko / hetã-ngo
   1PL.INCL subj      ja-         ña-           ja-guata / ña-ñoty
   reflexive/pass     je-         ñe-           je-juhu / ñe-mongeta
   reciprocal         jo-         ño-           jo-echa / ño-mongeta
   causative          mbo-        mo-           mbo-pu / mo-mýi
   2SG poss           nde-        ne-           nde róga / ne retã
   1PL.INCL poss      ñande-      ñane-         ñande róga / ñane retã
   2PL poss           pende-      pene-         pende róga / pene retã
   future (after V)   -ta         -mbota        oguata-ta / ohenduta (note: after pure
                                                 nasal V→nasal stem the m simplifies and -ta surfaces)
   ‘also’             avei        avei          (no alternation — fixed)

Key triggering examples for the LLM:
  mitã ‘child’ contains ã → NASAL → mitã-nguéra ‘children’, mitã-me ‘to the child’.
  jagua ‘dog’ has no nasal segment → ORAL → jagua-kuéra ‘dogs’, jagua-pe ‘to the dog’.
  óga ‘house’ → ORAL → óga-pe, óga-kuéra, ñande róga.
  tetã ‘country’ → NASAL → tetã-me, tetã-nguéra, ñane retã.

================================================================
C. SUBJECT PREFIXES — ACTIVE (areal) vs INACTIVE (chendal)
================================================================
Two distinct paradigms. Apertium tags them <ac> vs <in>.

ACTIVE (areal) — used by most action verbs (oguata, okaru, ohecha, oñeʼẽ, opuka):
   1SG       a-      a-guata        ‘I walk’
   2SG       re-     re-guata       ‘you walk’
   3         o-      o-guata        ‘s/he/it/they walks/walk’
   1PL.INCL  ja-/ña- ja-guata/ña-ñe’ẽ ‘we (incl.) walk/speak’
   1PL.EXCL  ro-     ro-guata       ‘we (excl.) walk’
   2PL       pe-     pe-guata       ‘you all walk’
   3PL       o-…(hikuái) o-guata hikuái ‘they walk’ (overt PL marker as auxiliary)

INACTIVE (chendal) — used by stative predicates, possessed nouns, and adjectival
predicates ("be-X", "have-X", body states, qualities). The same set is used as
OBJECT clitics on transitive verbs.
   1SG       che-              che-rasy        ‘I’m sick’,    che-róga ‘my house’
   2SG       nde-/ne-          nde-rasy/ne-mandu’a ‘you’re sick / you remember’
   3         i-/ij-/iñ-/h(i)-  i-porã / iñ-akã / h-esa ‘pretty / his head / his eye’
   1PL.INCL  ñande-/ñane-      ñande-rasy / ñane-retã
   1PL.EXCL  ore-              ore-rasy / ore-róga
   2PL       pende-/pene-      pende-rasy / pene-retã
   3PL       i-…(hikuái) / i-…-kuéra        i-porã hikuái / iporãva kuéra

3rd-person inactive/possessive allomorphs by stem onset:
   i-   before consonant:      i-jagua ‘his dog’, i-puku ‘it’s long’
   ij-  before oral vowel:     ij-ape ‘its surface’, ij-ao ‘his clothing’
   iñ-  before nasal vowel:    iñ-akã ‘his head’, iñ-emoĩ ‘his rival’
   hiʼ- / h- before tonal V:   hiʼ-ára ‘his day’, h-óga (rare; usually i-róga path)

ACTIVE vs INACTIVE — how to choose:
  * Action / volitional event   → ACTIVE (oguata, okaru, ohecha, oñeʼẽ, ojeroky).
  * State, quality, sensation,
    inalienable possession,
    weather                      → INACTIVE (iporã ‘is pretty’, hasy ‘hurts’,
                                              ipiru ‘is thin’, hetã ‘is many’,
                                              oky ‘rains’ is one ACTIVE exception).

================================================================
D. INALIENABLE NOUNS (T-/R-/H- triformes; also r-/h- biformes)
================================================================
Body parts, kinship, and a fixed list of relational nouns alternate the stem
onset by possessor. The CITATION form starts with t- (or r-, for biformes).

   citation  1/2-person poss    3-person poss      gloss
   tesa      che resa, nde resa  hesa              ‘eye’
   tape      che rape, nde rape  hape              ‘path/road’
   tetã      che retã, ñane retã hetã              ‘country’
   téra      che réra, nde réra  héra              ‘name’
   tova      che rova, nde rova  hova              ‘face’
   ru        che ru, nde ru      itúva (suppletive) ‘father’
   sy        che sy, nde sy      isy               ‘mother’
   raʼy      che raʼy, nde raʼy  taʼýra            ‘son’
   rajy      che rajy, nde rajy  tajýra            ‘daughter’
   tembireko che rembireko       hembireko         ‘wife’
   teko      che reko, nde reko  heko              ‘way of being, life’
   tembiʼu   che rembiʼu         hembiʼu           ‘food’
   tãi       che rãi, nde rãi    hãi               ‘tooth’
   po        che po, nde po      ipo               ‘hand’ (note: po is biforme, not triforme)
   apyka     che apyka           ijapyka           ‘chair’ (alienable — no t-/r-/h-!)

Rule of thumb: for ANY body part or close kinship relation, suspect a triforme.
For most other nouns, the simple alienable pattern (che + N, i + N) applies.

Biformes (only r- vs h-, no t-): óga ‘house’ → che róga, h-óga ‘his house’ (or
the more common path i-róga). Treat óga as the typical example.

================================================================
E. POSSESSIVE PREFIXES — full paradigm
================================================================
                ORAL stem (e.g., -óga, -jagua)   NASAL stem (e.g., -retã, -akã, -ñeʼẽ)
   1SG          che N            che róga       che N           che retã, che ñeʼẽ
   2SG          nde N            nde róga       ne N            ne retã, ne ñeʼẽ
   3            i N / ij V / hi(ʼ) Vstr   iróga, hiʼarapoty
                                              iñ Vnasal           iñakã
   1PL.INCL     ñande N          ñande róga    ñane N            ñane retã, ñane ñeʼẽ
   1PL.EXCL     ore N            ore róga      ore N             ore retã (no allomorph)
   2PL          pende N          pende róga    pene N            pene retã, pene ñeʼẽ
   3PL          i N + kuéra/hikuái                                iñakã kuéra

Alienable possessor inserts a linking r- before vowels: che r-aranduka ‘my book’,
nde r-ao ‘your clothing’.
For inalienable triformes the r- IS the stem allomorph (see §D).

================================================================
F. PERSONAL PRONOUNS (closed class — verbatim spellings; high chrF++ leverage)
================================================================
                NOM             DAT (-ve/-pe)        emphatic + EMPH
   1SG          che             chéve                cheko
   2SG          nde             ndéve                ndeko
   3SG          haʼe            ichupe / chupe       haʼeko
   1PL.INCL     ñande           ñandéve              ñandeko
   1PL.EXCL     ore             oréve                oreko
   2PL          peẽ             peẽme                peẽko
   3PL          haʼekuéra       chuguikuéra          haʼekuérako

ACCUSATIVE clitic forms on transitive verbs (= same as inactive set):
   che- nde- i-/ij-/iñ- ñande-/ñane- ore- pende-/pene- i-…-kuéra
Ex.:  o-che-juhu ‘he finds me’, ja-i-juhu ‘we (incl.) find him’.

DEMONSTRATIVE PRONOUNS (and demonstrative adjectives — same forms):
   SG  ko ‘this (near speaker)’    pe ‘that (near hearer)’    amo ‘that (yonder)’
       upe ‘that (anaphoric)’      ku ‘that (anaphoric)’      aipo ‘that (rumored)’
   PL  koʼã ‘these’                umi ‘those’                ã ‘these’
   ‘the one that…’: ko + N + va, koʼãva, umi…va  — relative-clausal use.

   ko jagua          ‘this dog’
   pe yvoty          ‘that flower’
   umi mitãnguéra    ‘those children’
   koʼã óga          ‘these houses’

================================================================
G. POSTPOSITIONS (closed class; precede no noun, attach AFTER it; surface allomorphs)
================================================================
SPATIAL / LOCATIVE
   -pe / -me     ‘in, at, to, on’      óga-pe, tetã-me, mesa-pe, ñu-me
   -gui          ‘from’                 ógagui, tetãgui (no nasal alternation)
   -ári / ári    ‘on top of’            mesa ári, óga ári
   -guý(pe)      ‘under’                yvyra guýpe ‘under the tree’
   -kupe(pe)     ‘behind’               óga kupépe
   -renonde      ‘in front of’          óga renonde
   -rovake       ‘in front of (face-to-face)’  che rovake
   -ykére        ‘beside’               che ykére
   -apytépe      ‘amongst’              haʼekuéra apytépe
   -mbytépe      ‘in the middle of’     yvyra mbytépe
   -rupi         ‘along, by way of’     tape rupi
   -guive        ‘from (origin)’         koʼág̃a guive
   -peve / -meve ‘until’ (-meve nasal)   pyhare peve

ASSOCIATIVE / COMITATIVE / INSTRUMENTAL
   -ndi / -ndive ‘with (accompaniment)’  che-ndive, ñande-ndive
   -re / -rehe   ‘about, on (subject of)’  ñeʼẽrehe ‘about a topic’
   -reheve       ‘together with’
   -rupive       ‘by means of’

PURPOSIVE / TEMPORAL
   -gua          ‘pertaining to, from’    Paraguái-gua ‘Paraguayan’
   -guarã        ‘for, intended for’      che guarã ‘for me’
   -hag̃ua       ‘in order to (CL)’       (verbal use)
   -mboyve       ‘before’                  ára mboyve
   -rire         ‘after’                   karu rire

PRIVATIVE
   -ʼỹre         ‘without’                 ñeʼẽ-ʼỹre

Postposition stacking is allowed: óga-pe-gua ‘from the house’, óga-pe-guarã ‘for the house’.

================================================================
H. NOMINAL TENSE — distinctive of Guarani (-kue, -rã)
================================================================
Nouns can take TENSE marking, independent of any verb.
   -kue   ‘former, ex-, used-to-be’       mburuvicha-kue ‘ex-leader’
                                           óga-kue ‘former house, ruin’
                                           che rembireko-kue ‘my ex-wife’
   -rã    ‘future, to-be, intended’       tembiʼu-rã ‘food (to-be-eaten)’
                                           mbaʼapo-rã ‘task (work-to-be-done)’
                                           tembireko-rã ‘fiancée (wife-to-be)’

These are FREQUENT and HIGHLY DISTINCTIVE strings. Apply them whenever the
caption refers to "the future X", "the former X", or "X for some purpose".

================================================================
I. VERBAL INFLECTION — tense, aspect, mood
================================================================
PRESENT is unmarked: o-guata ‘s/he walks / is walking’.
Captions of static scenes are almost always PRESENT.

FUTURE
   -ta            most general future            o-guata-ta ‘will walk’
   -mbota / -pota allomorph after some stems     o-jeʼe-mbota? o-mboʼe-pota
   -vaʼerã        ‘will / has to / is to’        o-guata vaʼerã

ASPECT / DISCOURSE PARTICLES (suffix-like, attached after verb)
   -ma     ‘already’                              o-guata-ma ‘he already walks/walked’
   -nte    ‘only, just’                           o-karu-nte ‘he only eats’
   -vo     ‘while, simultaneously’                o-guata-vo ‘while walking’
   -kuri   ‘recently (past)’                      o-guata-kuri ‘he just walked’
   -hína   ‘progressive (3rd p.)’                 o-guata hína ‘he is walking’
   -aína   1SG progressive                       a-guata-aína
   -reína  2SG progressive                       re-guata-reína
   hikuái  3PL progressive/auxiliary (always written separately)
                                                  o-guata hikuái ‘they walk’

NEGATION — discontinuous circumfix nd-...-i.
   nda- … -i      (oral consonant stems, 1SG/2SG/etc.)   nda-juhú-i ‘I don’t find’
   nde- … -i      (after some stems)                      ne-ñe’ẽ-i ‘he doesn’t speak’
   ndo- … -i      (3rd person + o-stem fusion)            ndo-guatá-i ‘he doesn’t walk’
   na-  … -i      (before nasal-initial stems)            na-mboʼé-i ‘he doesn’t teach’
The final -i becomes -i / -ri / -ʼi by twol; safe surface = -i.

QUESTION particles
   pa             general yes/no q.               oguata-pa? ‘does s/he walk?’
   piko           emphatic yes/no q.              mbaʼe piko? ‘what (is it)?’
                                                  mávapa? ‘who?’

================================================================
O. EXISTENTIAL / COPULA / IDENTIFICATION
================================================================
Two distinct copular strategies:
   oĩ            ‘there is, is located, exists’ — used for LOCATION and EXISTENCE.
                  oĩ ko jagua óga-pe.        ‘There is a dog in the house.’
                  oĩ heta yvoty ñu-me.       ‘There are many flowers in the field.’
   haʼe          ‘is, equals’ — used for IDENTIFICATION (X is a Y).
                  haʼe peteĩ kuña.           ‘She is a woman.’
                  ko mitã haʼe che raʼy.     ‘This child is my son.’
For ATTRIBUTIVE predication ("X is pretty"), use the inactive paradigm directly,
with NO copula:
                  ko jagua iporã.            ‘This dog is pretty.’
                  pe yvoty ipytã.            ‘That flower is red.’
Negation of haʼe: ndaha’ei. Negation of oĩ: ndaipóri.
                  ndaipóri jagua óga-pe.     ‘There is no dog in the house.’

================================================================
P. WORD ORDER (from rlx evidence + traditional grammars)
================================================================
1. SUBJECT VERB OBJECT is the default, but SUBJECT is usually PRO-DROP
   (recoverable from the prefix).
        a-hecha pe yvoty.                  ‘I see that flower.’  (1SG dropped)
        kuña ohecha mitãme.                ‘The woman sees the child.’  (overt S)
2. ADJECTIVES and most demonstrative-final markers FOLLOW the noun:
        jagua porã, jagua hũ, mitã michĩ, kuña pyahu.
3. ko/pe/amo/upe/ku (preposed demonstratives) and umi/koʼã (preposed PL dem.)
   PRECEDE the noun. Numerals also precede.
        ko jagua porã. mokõi mitã. umi yvoty pytã.
4. POSSESSOR precedes POSSESSUM:
        mitã sy ‘the child’s mother’,    che jagua porã ‘my pretty dog’.
5. POSTPOSITIONAL phrases generally FOLLOW the verb, but can be fronted:
        ja-guata tape rupi.                ‘We walk along the road.’
        ko óga-pe oĩ heta mitã.            ‘In this house there are many children.’
6. COORDINATION uses ha ‘and’, térã ‘or’, katu ‘but’:
        kuña ha kuimbaʼe. yvoty térã yvyra. tuicha katu vai.
7. RELATIVE / "the one which" uses -va (after V) or umi…va (DET + N + va):
        oguata-va ‘the one walking’.       umi mitã o-pukáva ‘the children who laugh’.

================================================================
Q. PLURAL — IMPORTANT: it’s OPTIONAL
================================================================
Guarani does not require number marking. Bare nouns are number-neutral and very
frequently translate as plural in context. Use -kuéra/-nguéra ONLY when:
- you really want to emphasise plurality (especially for HUMANS), and/or
- there is no other plural cue (no numeral, no umi, no quantifier).
Avoid double-marking with numerals: mokõi jagua, NOT *mokõi jaguakuéra.

   jagua kuéra okaru.               ‘The dogs eat.’ (plural emphasis)
   ohecha mitã.                     ‘He sees a child / children.’ (number-neutral)
   heta mitã ovyʼa.                 ‘Many children are happy.’ (no -kuéra needed)

================================================================
S. PRODUCTIVE COMPOUNDING (very common in image captioning)
================================================================
Guarani readily compounds N+N or N+ADJ. Prefer compounds over postpositional
descriptions when a lexical compound exists:
   mitã + kuña → mitãkuña ‘girl’
   mbarakaja + kuña → mbarakaja kuña ‘female cat’
   óga + ruvicha → óga ruvicha ‘house master/owner’ (rare)
   y + voty → yvoty? No — yvoty is its own root (‘flower’).
   ao + jyva → ao jyva ‘sleeve’
   pe + akã → peakã (no — productive only with stems already lexicalised)
For body-part compounds: stem + body-part is common, e.g., kavaju akã ‘horse head’.
For colour-modified nouns, just postpose: jagua hũ ‘black dog’, yvoty pytã ‘red flower’.

================================================================
U. DEFAULT RULES OF THUMB
================================================================
1. UNCERTAIN about an affix allomorph? Scan the stem for {ã ẽ ĩ õ ũ ỹ ñ m n}.
   Any present → nasal allomorph. None → oral allomorph.
2. UNCERTAIN whether a verb is active or inactive? Test: does the predicate
   describe an event the subject performs (active) or a state the subject
   undergoes/has (inactive)? "He sees" = active (ohecha). "He is sick" =
   inactive (hasy chupe / che rasy).
3. UNCERTAIN about plural? Bare noun is fine if context is clear or there is
   a numeral/quantifier. Use -kuéra/-nguéra for explicit human plurals.
4. UNCERTAIN whether to copy a Spanish loan? Prefer native (jagua, kuña, óga).
   Use Apertium-listed loans (kavaju, vaka, ovecha, kavara, aviõ, ijapyka)
   when no native counterpart exists in the cheat-sheet.
5. UNCERTAIN about word order? Default to: (Numeral/Det) Noun (Adj) (PostP) V (Obj).
   Subject can be dropped if the prefix on V identifies it.
6. UNCERTAIN about an irregular verb? Listed irregulars: aha/reho/oho ‘go’,
   aju/reju/ou ‘come’, haʼu/reʼu/hoʼu ‘eat (food)’, hayʼu/reyʼu/hoyʼu ‘drink’,
   haʼe/eré/heʼi ‘say’.
7. KEEP captions short (one clause). chrF++ is character-level: small clauses
   give the model more chances to match exact frequent substrings (-pe, -kuéra,
   o-, i-, ko, umi, peteĩ, haʼe, oĩ).
