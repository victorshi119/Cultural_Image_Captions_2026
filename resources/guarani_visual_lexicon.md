# Visual Lexicon: Guaraní for Image Captioning

## Food & Drink

- steamed cheese-and-starch bread → **chipa** (Spanish: chipa) — loanword into Spanish; same form in Guaraní
- cornmeal cake / "Paraguayan soup" → **sopa paraguáya** (Spanish: sopa paraguaya) — solid cornbread, not a liquid soup
- starch griddle cake → **mbeju** (Spanish: mbejú)
- broth with corn-and-cheese dumplings → **vori vori** (Spanish: vori vori)
- cassava / manioc root → **mandi'o** (Spanish: mandioca)
- corn (the plant or kernels) → **avati** (Spanish: maíz)
- cheese → **kesu** (Spanish: queso) — Spanish loan, common in caption use
- meat → **so'o** (Spanish: carne)
- beef stew with vegetables → **so'o josopy** (Spanish: guiso de carne) — traditional Paraguayan dish
- water → **y** (Spanish: agua)
- cold yerba mate infusion → **terere** (Spanish: tereré)
- hot yerba mate infusion → **ka'ay** (Spanish: mate) — literally "yerba water"
- yerba mate (the plant/leaf) → **ka'a** (Spanish: yerba mate)
- hollowed gourd cup for tereré/mate → **guampa** (Spanish: guampa)
- metal drinking straw with filter → **mbomba** (Spanish: bombilla)
- thermos for cold water → **termo** (Spanish: termo) — Spanish loan, near-universal
- bread → **mbujape** (Spanish: pan)
- salt → **juky** (Spanish: sal)
- egg → **ryguasu rupi'a** (Spanish: huevo de gallina)
- milk → **kamby** (Spanish: leche)
- honey → **eira** (Spanish: miel)

## Plants & Crops

- medicinal herbs (general) → **pohã ñana** (Spanish: yuyos / hierbas medicinales) — sold and used daily, often pounded for tereré
- tree → **yvyra** (Spanish: árbol)
- flower → **yvoty** (Spanish: flor)
- leaf → **hogue** (Spanish: hoja)
- forest / scrubland → **ka'aguy** (Spanish: monte / bosque)
- field / cultivated plot → **kokue** (Spanish: chacra)
- garden / yard → **kogape** (Spanish: huerta)

## Animals

- animal (general) → **mymba** (Spanish: animal)
- bird → **guyra** (Spanish: pájaro / ave)
- parrot → **tu'ĩ** (Spanish: loro / cotorra)
- chicken / hen → **ryguasu** (Spanish: gallina)
- rooster → **ryguasu me** (Spanish: gallo)
- dog → **jagua** (Spanish: perro)
- cat → **mbarakaja** (Spanish: gato)
- cow → **vaka** (Spanish: vaca) — Spanish loan, standard in modern usage
- horse → **kavaju** (Spanish: caballo)
- pig → **kure** (Spanish: cerdo / chancho)
- fish → **pira** (Spanish: pez / pescado)
- snake → **mbói** (Spanish: serpiente / víbora)
- jaguar → **jaguarete** (Spanish: jaguar / yaguareté)
- capybara → **kapi'yva** (Spanish: carpincho)
- armadillo → **tatu** (Spanish: armadillo / tatú)

## Textiles & Crafts

- spider-web–style needle lace → **ñanduti** (Spanish: ñandutí) — literally "spiderweb"; circular medallions
- fine embroidered cotton cloth → **ao po'i** (Spanish: ao po'í) — "ropa fina"; light embroidered shirts/blouses
- clothing (general) → **ao** (Spanish: ropa)
- hammock → **kyha** (Spanish: hamaca)
- straw hat → **akãngao** (Spanish: sombrero) — broader: head covering
- basket → **ajaka** (Spanish: canasta / cesto)
- clay pot → **japepo** (Spanish: olla de barro)

## Music & Dance

- Paraguayan harp → **arpa** (Spanish: arpa paraguaya) — Spanish loan; the iconic instrument
- guitar → **mbaraka** (Spanish: guitarra) — also can refer to a rattle/percussion in older usage; in modern Paraguayan Guaraní commonly means guitar
- song → **purahéi** (Spanish: canción)
- dance → **jeroky** (Spanish: baile / danza)
- dancing → **ojeroky hína** (Spanish: está bailando)
- bottle dance (woman balancing bottles) → **jeroky mbayru reheve** (Spanish: danza de la botella) — descriptive phrase

## Rural Life

- house → **óga** (Spanish: casa)
- traditional homestead / way of life → **tekoha** (Spanish: territorio / lugar de vida) — carries strong cultural weight; use when scene clearly shows a community space
- countryside → **okára** (Spanish: campo / afueras)
- road / path → **tape** (Spanish: camino)
- river → **ysyry** (Spanish: río)
- fire → **tata** (Spanish: fuego)
- earth / ground / land → **yvy** (Spanish: tierra)
- sun → **kuarahy** (Spanish: sol)
- rain → **ama** (Spanish: lluvia)
- night → **pyhare** (Spanish: noche)
- day → **ára** (Spanish: día)

## Urban / Market

- market → **mercado** (Spanish: mercado) — Spanish loan dominates; "ñemuha" exists but is less common in caption-style speech
- street → **tape** (Spanish: calle) — same word as path; context disambiguates
- money → **viru** (Spanish: dinero / plata) — colloquial; "pirapire" is more formal
- to sell → **oñemu** (Spanish: vende) — "está vendiendo": *oñemu hína*
- to buy → **ojogua** (Spanish: compra)

## People & Actions

- woman → **kuña** (Spanish: mujer)
- man → **kuimba'e** (Spanish: hombre)
- child → **mitã** (Spanish: niño/a)
- elder / grandparent → **tujami / jaryi** (Spanish: anciano / abuela)
- family → **tetãygua / ogapy** (Spanish: familia) — *ogapy* = household members
- they are sharing tereré → **oñomboja'o terere hikuái** (Spanish: están compartiendo tereré)
- she is drinking tereré → **ho'u terere** (Spanish: está tomando tereré)
- she is weaving ñandutí → **ojapo ñanduti** (Spanish: está haciendo ñandutí) — literally "is making ñandutí"
- he is playing the harp → **ombopu arpa** (Spanish: está tocando el arpa) — *mbopu* = make sound
- she is cooking → **okaru apo** / **ojapo tembi'u** (Spanish: está cocinando) — *ojapo tembi'u* = "makes food"
- they are eating → **okaru hikuái** (Spanish: están comiendo)
- she is selling at the market → **oñemu mercado-pe** (Spanish: vende en el mercado)
- they are sitting together → **oguapy oñondive** (Spanish: están sentados juntos)
- he is working in the field → **omba'apo kokue-pe** (Spanish: trabaja en la chacra)
- she is carrying a basket → **ogueraha ajaka** (Spanish: lleva un canasto)
- the children are playing → **mitãnguéra oñembosarái** (Spanish: los niños juegan)

## Uncertain / verify before use

**Lower-confidence entries included above:**
- **mbaraka** for "guitar" — historically refers to a rattle; modern usage as "guitar" is common but not universal. Verify with a Paraguayan speaker before relying on it in captions.
- **akãngao** for "hat" — literally "head-cloth"; widely understood but *sombrero* (Spanish loan) may appear more often in real captions.
- **tetãygua** for "family" — more accurately means "compatriots/community members"; *ogapy* is safer for the household sense. Consider just using *ogapy* or the Spanish loan *familia*.
- **viru** for "money" — colloquial/slang register; fine for everyday scenes, less appropriate for formal captions.
- **eira** for "honey" — correct but can also mean "bee"; context usually makes it clear.
- **jeroky mbayru reheve** for "bottle dance" — descriptive construction rather than an attested fixed term; a real caption would more likely use the Spanish *danza de la botella*.

**Concepts from the source document deliberately skipped** (no reliable single Guaraní term suitable for visual captioning, or only meaningful in non-visual/spiritual contexts):
- **Jopara** — refers to the mixed Spanish-Guaraní register itself; not a visual object.
- **teko porã** — abstract concept of collective wellbeing; not depictable.
- **Pohã ñana preparation in a mortar** — "angu'a" (mortar) and "angu'a memby" (pestle) exist but I'm not confident enough in their everyday caption usage to include without verification.
- **All mythological figures** (Pombero, Jasy Jateré, Kurupí, Ao Ao, Luisón, Karai Octubre, Plata Yvyguy, Teju Jagua, Mbói Tu'ĩ, Moñái) — these are oral-tradition entities, generally not depicted in everyday photographs. If they appear in stylized illustrations, the names themselves serve as the caption term.
- **Guarania / polka paraguaya** — music genres, not visually distinguishable from a generic music scene without contextual cues; safest to caption the visible instruments and action instead.
- **Specific religious syncretism imagery** — "tupãópe" (in the church) and "Tupã" (God/thunder deity) exist but their caption-appropriate use depends heavily on scene specifics.
